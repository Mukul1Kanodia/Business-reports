India Macro Transmission
CAD, Rupee, RBI: The Macro Transmission Chain
How a crude oil shock travels through India's economy — from import bill to sector margins
Every $10/bbl rise in Brent crude adds approximately $14–15B to India's annual import bill — mechanically widening the Current Account Deficit by ~0.4–0.5% of GDP, given India's ~85% import dependence on crude.
A wider CAD creates structural dollar demand, applying depreciation pressure on the INR. RBI's intervention capacity is finite — each dollar of reserve drawdown buys currency stability at the cost of import cover adequacy.
Goldman Sachs cut India's GDP forecast to 5.9% and flagged a 50 bps rate hike risk, citing energy-driven inflationary pressure. The policy trilemma forces RBI to choose between inflation control, currency stability, and growth — it cannot win on all three simultaneously.
The shock lands asymmetrically at the sector level. Aviation, paints, and OMCs face direct cost-side compression. IT and pharma benefit as dollar earners. The master analytical lens: is this company a crude cost-taker or a dollar earner?

Section 01
The Origin Signal: Crude Oil

India's relationship with crude oil is structurally asymmetric: it produces roughly 15% of its requirement domestically and imports the remaining ~85%, making it a pure price-taker in global oil markets. Unlike China or the US, India has no supply-side lever. Every dollar move in Brent is a direct pass-through to the import bill.

Brent peaked at $100.93/bbl in 2022 following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, then moderated to $80.52 in 2024. The pandemic year (2020) produced the decade's trough at $41.96 — briefly turning crude-linked import savings into a macroeconomic cushion. India's import bill tracked this cycle with near-mechanical precision, moving from $64B (FY2016) to $157B (FY2022) and back toward $145B (FY2024).

"A supply shock India cannot avoid, cannot hedge at the sovereign level, and cannot substitute at scale. The only question is how far down the chain the damage travels — and how fast."
Section 02
The Accumulation Chamber: Current Account Deficit

The Current Account Deficit is where the crude shock accumulates before becoming visible in FX markets. India's goods trade deficit widens mechanically with every oil price spike, partially offset by a growing services surplus (IT/BPO exports) and remittances. But structural drivers — particularly cheap Russian crude access and software export growth — have created notable exceptions to the simple crude-CAD link.

Exhibit 3: India CAD as % of GDP (FY2015–FY2024) — crude is the primary driver, but structural factors create meaningful divergences
Brent crude (USD/bbl) CAD (% of GDP)
CAD ranged from +0.9% surplus in FY21 (pandemic) to -2.1% in FY19 and -2.0% in FY23. Brent peaked at $101 in FY22.
Source: Reserve Bank of India, Handbook of Statistics on Indian Economy (Balance of Payments, annual); PPAC / MacroTrends for Brent crude.

Three episodes deserve specific note. FY2020–21 produced India's only modern surplus (+0.9% of GDP) as pandemic lockdowns collapsed imports while crude crashed. FY2022–23 saw the deficit widen to –2.0% as the Ukraine war spiked global energy costs. FY2023–24 moderated sharply to –0.7% — driven not by falling crude, but by cheaper discounted Russian crude imports and accelerating services exports, demonstrating that the crude-CAD relationship, while structural, is not deterministic.

Exhibit 4: India's CAD decomposition — the services surplus is a partial but growing shock absorber

Goods deficit widened in FY22-23 to over 250B. Services surplus grew to 150B+ by FY24. Net CAD peaked at -80B in FY23.

A widening CAD does not stay on paper. It shows up immediately in one place — the foreign exchange market, where dollar demand from oil importers meets a finite supply of reserves.
Section 03
Where the Market Prices the Damage: The Rupee

The INR is India's real-time stress indicator for external account pressure. A widening CAD generates structural dollar demand from oil importers. This demand, if unmet by RBI intervention, drives INR depreciation. However, the relationship between crude price levels and the INR is not always contemporaneous — it is mediated by RBI intervention intensity, global risk sentiment, and capital flow cycles.

Exhibit 5: INR/USD trajectory (annual average) vs RBI forex reserves — intervention explains why crude-INR correlation is imperfect
Note: The INR does not depreciate proportionally with every crude spike because RBI actively intervenes using forex reserves. The correct analytical chain is: crude rises → CAD widens → dollar demand increases → RBI deploys reserves to defend INR → reserves fall. The rupee depreciation becomes visible only when reserve capacity is strained or RBI chooses to let the currency adjust.
INR/USD (annual avg, LHS) Forex reserves (USD B, RHS)
INR depreciated steadily from 64 in 2015 to 83 in 2024. Reserves peaked at 642B in Sept 2021, drew down to 524B in Oct 2022 as RBI defended the rupee during the Ukraine shock.
Source: RBI Reference Rate (annual average); RBI Weekly Statistical Supplement, Section 4: Foreign Exchange Reserves. The 2021–22 reserve drawdown of ~$118B directly corresponds to RBI's active defense of the rupee during the crude and dollar strength shock of that period.

The critical insight: between September 2021 and October 2022, RBI deployed approximately $118B in reserves — a drawdown of ~18% — to slow the rupee's fall. Every dollar spent on currency defense reduced India's import cover from ~15 months to ~9 months. This trade-off is precisely what forces the policy decision in the next section.

Section 04
The Impossible Triangle: RBI's Policy Response

Once the rupee is under pressure and inflation is rising, RBI faces a trilemma it cannot resolve cleanly. In 2022, it raised the repo rate by 250 bps in under a year — the most aggressive tightening cycle since the 2013 taper tantrum. Goldman Sachs' current India forecast reflects the same trade-off being priced in again.

"Goldman Sachs revised its India GDP forecast to 5.9% for 2026, citing energy price-driven inflationary pressures and flagging a 50 basis point rate hike risk from the Reserve Bank of India as the central bank navigates between currency defense and growth support."— Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research, India Economics, April 2026 (as reported by Economic Times / Bloomberg)

Inflation control
Rate hikes suppress demand-pull inflation. Crude feeds CPI directly via fuel (6.8% weight) and transport.
Currency stability
Rate hikes attract capital inflows, supporting INR. Reserve drawdown provides direct FX defense.
Growth support
Sacrificed. Rate hikes raise cost of capital. Goldman Sachs cuts India GDP to 5.9%. Corporate margins compress.
Source: Illustrative framework based on RBI MPC Minutes, June–September 2022; Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research, India Economics, April 2026.
Exhibit 8: RBI repo rate vs Brent crude (FY2015–FY2024) — rate response follows crude with a 1–2 quarter lag, but growth concerns can override
Repo rate % (LHS, stepped) Brent crude USD/bbl (RHS)
Repo rate cut from 7.5% in FY15 to 4.0% during COVID in FY21, then hiked sharply to 6.5% by FY23 tracking the Ukraine crude spike. Held at 6.5% in FY24 as crude moderated.
Source: RBI MPC decisions archive (repo rate at fiscal year-end); PPAC / MacroTrends for Brent annual averages. FY2020–21 rate cuts reflect pandemic growth prioritization despite subsequent crude rebound — illustrating that growth concerns can temporarily override the inflation-rate hike linkage.

The FY2021–22 data point is analytically important: despite crude recovering to $79.50/bbl from the pandemic trough, RBI held rates at 4.0% — deliberately accepting the inflation risk to nurse the post-COVID recovery. It was only in May 2022, after CPI breached 7%, that RBI pivoted to aggressive tightening (+250 bps by February 2023). This lag is the empirical basis for Goldman Sachs' current 50 bps hike risk assessment.

Higher rates don't stay in the bond market. A 250 bps hiking cycle raises the cost of capital for every corporate borrower in India — and that's precisely where macro becomes micro.
Section 05
Where Macro Becomes Micro: Corporate Margins & Sector Scorecard

The crude shock reaches corporate India through two independent channels: direct cost-side exposure (for companies with crude-linked input costs) and currency translation effects (for companies with dollar revenues). These channels cut in opposite directions — creating clear winners and losers that can be mapped with a single analytical lens.

Aviation is the starkest case: ATF comprises 30–35% of IndiGo's operating expenses. A sustained $20/bbl increase in crude translates to ~₹3,000–4,000 crore in additional annual fuel costs — nearly eliminating operating profit at current margins. IT services flip the equation: TCS and Infosys both reported 40–80 bps of EBIT margin benefit in FY2022–23 commentary explicitly attributing it to rupee depreciation on dollar-denominated contracts.

The oil-shock transmission logic applies directly to India, with currency and monetary policy as additional nodes absent in the European case.
The Reader's Checklist — When Crude Moves, Ask In Sequence
01What does this do to India's import bill? ($14–15B per $10/bbl is the anchor figure.)
02How does the CAD respond? Are there structural offsets (Russian crude discount, services surplus growth)?
03Where does INR go — and how much reserve cover does RBI have? Import cover months is the key ratio.
04What is the MPC's inflation-growth trade-off? Is CPI already above the 6% upper band?
05Which sectors are crude cost-takers? Which are dollar earners? This single lens handles most sector-level analysis.
